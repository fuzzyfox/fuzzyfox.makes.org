KIJ13 Mozilla Webmaker Hackjam

5 Hour Agenda

[10 mins] Introductions

Start by introducing yourself and explaining what YOU want them to learn. Include links to the workshop etherpad and explain that you're keeping that pad to stay connected, catalog links and share work.

To keep introductions short, use a prompt that allows people to say a single unique thing. For example, "My name is, I work on/for/at, and if you really knew me, you'd know..."

A Facilitator has to be willing to make a fool out of him/herself, so choose a game to loosen people up and be animated in the way you begin. People are self conscious, show them that you see everyone as 100% awesome. Try to remain positive the entire time. When you have "problem people" be firm, but nice, and remember you're the workshop leaders.

[20 mins] Spectrogram Icebreaker

The Spectrogram uses short, controversial or extreme statements to begin a discussion about the topics of the day.

How to spectrogram

Place post its with numbers from one to ten written on them, in a line on the floor or wall. Leave enough space for people to separate along the line.

Explain the activity: You will read a statement, and if they completelt agree with that statement, they should stand near the number 10. If they completely disagree, they should stand near the number 1. Those who are unsure, or only half agree should stand at number 5. Tell the participants that they can change their minds based on what others say.

Read the first statement and once participants have divided themselves along the line, ask someone why they are standing where they are. Use the activity to begin discussion and debate on the essential themes of the day.

Allow learners to explore ideas during the activity. At the end, tie learners comments and ideas together with the key topics of the event.

[10 mins] Break into Groups

No one has to work alone. They will work in pairs, but with their peers at the table. Oh, sounds impossible? No, participants get to ask their neighbours to help them instead of raising their hand. This isn't primary school! Explain that you'll be floating and help people that get stuck. Tell people to work with those they don't know.

Persona and the Badge Backback

Get everyone to set up with Persona and their Webmaker Space

[30 mins] MAKE - Getting to know the tools

Before they go diving into making their project having never used the tools before we're going to spend a little time getting to know how Popcorn Maker and Thimble work by remixing some of the starter makes available from webmaker.org.

This will allow them to familiarise themesleves with the tools, as well as make some extra goodies to share with their friends and family after the hackjam.

You float. Be sure to point people to additional resources, websites or cheatsheets!

[40 mins] Primary Make Introduction

Regroup after the last activity and explain to everyone what YOU want them to build. Explain why they've been getting footage all week, and what they should try and make, as well as the benefits to their troop.

[30 mins (of the 40)] Stickynote Storyboarding

Splitting back into pairs (this time in pairs from the same troop), explain that all great videos are planned a little before their made. Briefly describe the value of a storyboard, as well as what they should be putting into theirs.

YOU will be floating, encouraging them to share their ideas with others at the table, and taking photos of their finished storyboards for later on.

Stickynote Storyboarding

Stickynote Storyboarding is almost identical to the regular kind, except more fun, more creative, and way more remixable. In place of the usual frames on the standard storyboard template. Notes about each frame can then be written under the stickynote instead of under.

[10 mins] Robot Dance

This is a great way to inject a little more energy into the group, and can be used to physically demonstrate what it means to hack.

How to do the robot dance

  • Form a circle and practice a robot dance together. Keep it simple though.
  • Now start hacking the dance. The first person in the cicle changes a step, then demonstrates the change.
  • The person to the left dances the new step, and either hacks that step, or hacks another step.
  • Repeat until everyone has danced. After the last person has made their hack, everyone practices the new dance together.

[20 mins] Mesh Icebreaker

Time to warm up again, recap the morning, and re-introduce/remind what the primary make is all about.

How to Mesh

To setup the activity get all participants standing in a circle. Holding onto one end of the string/yarn, toss the ball to someone in the group and ask them a question about their morning/experience at KIJ13.

Whomever catches the ball gives their answer to the question and then asks a new question. Then, the learner asks a new question and tosses the ball to someone new. The discussion continues until everyone has answered at least one question.

[1hr 30 mins] MAKE - Primary Make

Everyone splits into their (troop) pairs. A practical, project based activity allows learners to delve deeper into the essential questions of your session.

Break into pairs
  • In pairs, revisit the storyboards from earlier, and make any last changes.
Start Hacking
  • Focus on the idea(s) that got the most traction, using the paper prototype storyboards as a reminder. This helps formulate final ideas and exposes any holes.
  • There's no rush to finish this, however for those that finish quickly you can suggest ways to remix, and extend their make, or give them a sneeky peak at the next activity so they can start brainstorming ideas on how to complete that.

[40 mins] BONUS: Thimble Hacking!

This can be skipped if people want to finish their popcorn makes.

Working from the premade template people should roundup their day. They can include their popcorn makes, thimble projects they've worked on, and what they feel they've learned! As a bonus they could remix this project to express their thoughts, and experiences of KIJ13 as a whole!

Once their Thimble make is complete they should share it out with their firends and family to let them know how much fun they've missed out on by not going to camp!

You should float, provide... you get the idea by now :P

[20 mins] A Strong Wind Blows

Help clarify topics covered, skills leaned and best moments, with this hack on the well known game of musical chairs.

Thematic statements are introduced and learners for whom the statement is true, must find a new seat.

How to play

To setup the game, get all participants sitting in a circle with no empty chairs. The facilitator, who is standing, is the first to play. Tell the learners that the topic is the hackjam and the web. The facilitator makes the first statement. E.G.

  • "A Strong wind blows for anyone who knows HTML syntax"
  • "A Strong wind blows for anyone who knows what CSS stands for"
  • "A Strong wind blows for anyone who thinks Wikipedia is always true"

As soon as the statement has been made, every person for whom the statement is true must jump out of their seat and find a new available seat. The person who made the statement is almost always able to grab a seat, which leaves a new person standing once all seats are filled. That person must then make a "strong wind blows" statement to continue the game.

An additional rule is provided for amusement and chaos, as well as for anyone unable to fashion a "strong wind blows" statment: the person standing may yell "Hurricane!", which compels all participants to scramble for a new seat.

When the session facilitator is ready for the game to close (after perhaps 10 - 15 minutes), they simply stand and refrain from grabbing a seat when other participants are changing position.

Bonus points

When playing this game at the end of the event, spend a few minutes at the end discussing the links between peoples responses now, to the icebreaker you ran at the begining.

[30 min] Closing Circle

Use the closing circle to celebrate what you made. Have participants present their prototypes and encourage them to think about what they want to do next with their project.

If you like (and there is time/the ability to), share your prototypes with the world! You can publish the links and other materials to a blog or the social network of your choice.
